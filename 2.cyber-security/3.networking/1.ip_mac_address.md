# Networking Fundamentals: IP vs. MAC Address

## 1. MAC Address (Media Access Control)
**The Concept (What & Why):**
- **Layer:** Data Link Layer (Layer 2).
- **Definition:** The physical, hardcoded address of a device's Network Interface Card (NIC).
- **Format:** 6 pairs of hexadecimal digits (e.g., `00:1A:2B:3C:4D:5E`).
- **Scope:** Operates *only* within a Local Area Network (LAN). It **never** crosses a router to enter the public internet.
- **Analogy:** It is "Who you are" (Your fixed identity, like your name).

**The Execution (How to check):**
- **Windows:** Open Command Prompt (`cmd`) and type `ipconfig /all`. Look for "Physical Address".
- **Linux/Mac:** Open terminal and type `ifconfig` or `ip a`. Look for "ether".

**The Defense/Impact (So What?):**
- **Red Team Perspective:** Hackers use tools to spoof (fake) their MAC address to hide their device identity or perform **ARP Spoofing/Man-in-the-Middle (MITM)** attacks within a local network (intercepting traffic by pretending to be the router).
- **Blue Team Perspective:** Network administrators use MAC Filtering to block unauthorized devices physically or track compromised machines inside internal networks.

## 2. IP Address (Internet Protocol)
**The Concept (What & Why):**
- **Layer:** Network Layer (Layer 3).
- **Definition:** The logical address assigned to a device connected to a network, used for routing data across different networks (the internet).
- **Scope:** Operates globally (Public IP) or locally (Private IP).
- **Analogy:** It is "Where you are" (Your current location/address). If you move to another network, your IP changes.

**Types of IP Addresses:**
1. **Local/Private IP:** Assigned by your home/office router (e.g., `192.168.1.5`). Free, private, and not directly accessible from the internet. Used for internal routing.
2. **Public IP:** Assigned by your Internet Service Provider (ISP). Unique globally across the entire internet. This is what the internet (e.g., Google, Facebook) sees when you browse.

**Is it fixed?**
- No. Most IPs are **Dynamic** (change upon router restart or lease expiry via DHCP). However, you can buy a **Static IP** which remains unchanged.

**The Execution (How to check):**
- **Local IP (Windows):** Run `ipconfig` in cmd (Look for IPv4 Address).
- **Public IP:** Search "What is my IP" on Google.

**The Defense/Impact (So What?):**
- **Red Team Perspective:** Hackers need a target's Public IP to scan for open ports remotely (e.g., using Nmap) and launch exploits over the internet.
- **Blue Team Perspective:** If an attack occurs from the outside, the firewall will log the attacker's Public IP. Defenders block this IP or track the ISP origin to trace the attacker.

---

## 3. The Relationship: Why Do We Need Both?
When you send a packet from your computer to an internet server (like Facebook):
1. The **IP Addresses (Source and Destination)** act like addresses sealed inside an envelope. They remain constant from start to finish.
2. The **MAC Addresses (Source and Destination)** act like tracking stickers on the outside of the envelope. At every stop (Router/ISP), the old MAC is erased, and a new MAC pair is stamped for the next hop.

*Without an IP, data wouldn't know its final global destination. Without a MAC, data wouldn't know how to take the very first step out of the local network.*
